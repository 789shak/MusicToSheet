DMCA Copyright Policy

Last Updated: April 2026  ·  Pursuant to 17 U.S.C. § 512 (Digital Millennium Copyright Act)

Music To Sheet respects the intellectual property rights of others and expects users to do the same. This policy explains how we handle copyright infringement claims, our DMCA Safe Harbor procedures, and our repeat infringer enforcement policy.

1 Introduction

Music To Sheet is committed to complying with the Digital Millennium Copyright Act ("DMCA"), 17 U.S.C. § 512. As an audio-to-sheet-music conversion service, we take copyright concerns seriously. We process audio ephemerally — files are permanently deleted immediately after conversion — but we recognize that the output generated from copyrighted material may itself raise copyright questions.

If you believe that content accessible through our Service infringes your copyright, please follow the procedures described in this policy.

2 DMCA Safe Harbor Statement

Music To Sheet qualifies for safe harbor protection under the DMCA, 17 U.S.C. § 512. As a condition of that protection, we have designated a copyright agent to receive notices of claimed infringement, we respond expeditiously to valid takedown notices, and we maintain and enforce a repeat infringer policy as required by 17 U.S.C. § 512(i).

Note on ephemeral audio: User-uploaded audio files are permanently deleted from our servers immediately after each conversion. At the time of a takedown request, we may no longer hold the source audio. We will act on valid notices by disabling access to any stored output (such as saved sheet music) and, where applicable, restricting the account from re-processing the same content.

3 Filing a DMCA Takedown Notice

If you believe that content accessible through Music To Sheet infringes your copyright, you may submit a written takedown notice to our designated agent. To be valid under 17 U.S.C. § 512(c)(3), your notice must include all of the following elements:

  1. Signature — A physical or electronic signature of a person authorized to act on behalf of the owner of the copyright that has allegedly been infringed.
  2. Identification of the copyrighted work — Identification of the copyrighted work claimed to have been infringed (e.g., song title, composer, recording artist, label, ISRC or ISWC where known).
  3. Identification of the infringing material — Identification of the specific material claimed to be infringing or the subject of infringing activity, with sufficient detail to locate it (e.g., track name, account identifier, description of the generated sheet music output).
  4. Contact information — Your full name, mailing address, telephone number, and email address.
  5. Good faith statement — A statement that you have a good faith belief that use of the material in the manner complained of is not authorized by the copyright owner, its agent, or the law.
  6. Accuracy statement — A statement, made under penalty of perjury, that the information in your notice is accurate, and that you are the copyright owner or authorized to act on the copyright owner's behalf.
Warning: Knowingly submitting a materially false DMCA notice may expose you to liability for damages, including attorneys' fees, under 17 U.S.C. § 512(f). Only file a notice if you have a genuine, good-faith belief that infringement has occurred.

4 Counter-Notification Process

If content or output you created was removed or disabled in response to a DMCA notice and you believe the removal was made in error — for example, because you hold the necessary rights or because your use qualifies as fair use — you may file a counter-notification with our designated DMCA agent.

A valid counter-notification under 17 U.S.C. § 512(g)(3) must include:

  1. Your physical or electronic signature.
  2. Identification of the material that was removed or disabled, and the location at which it appeared before it was removed.
  3. A statement under penalty of perjury that you have a good-faith belief that the material was removed or disabled as a result of mistake or misidentification.
  4. Your full name, mailing address, and telephone number.
  5. A statement that you consent to the jurisdiction of the federal district court for your judicial district (or, if outside the United States, any judicial district in which Music To Sheet may be found), and that you will accept service of process from the person who submitted the original takedown notice.

Upon receipt of a valid counter-notification, we will forward it to the original complainant and, absent a court order, restore or re-enable the content within 10–14 business days.

6 Fair Use and Copyright Exceptions

Nothing in this policy limits or abridges any rights you may have under fair use (US), fair dealing (UK, Canada, Australia), or other applicable copyright exceptions in your jurisdiction. We recognize that personal study, commentary, criticism, and educational transcription may qualify for copyright exceptions in many jurisdictions.

If you believe your use qualifies as fair use or another applicable exception, you may include this information in any counter-notification you submit. We will consider such information in good faith.
